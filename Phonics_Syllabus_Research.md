# Research-Backed Phonics Syllabus for Ages 3 to 6

A 10-module sequence built from the four most authoritative phonics frameworks
in current use, with citations.

---

## How this syllabus was assembled

I cross-referenced four evidence-based programmes:

1. **UK Letters and Sounds** (Department for Education, 2007) — six-phase national
   framework, used in most UK primaries, fully aligned with the Science of Reading.
   [Letters and Sounds — full PDF](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/5a7aa7b6e5274a34770e630c/Letters_and_Sounds_-_DFES-00281-2007.pdf)
2. **Jolly Phonics** (Lloyd & Wernham, 1992; current edition) — the
   multisensory programme used in 100+ countries, organises 42 sounds into 7
   teaching groups in a kid-friendly order.
   [Jolly Education scope and sequence (PDF)](https://jollyeducation.com.au/uploads/1/2/1/5/121595094/scope_and_sequence_foundation_year.pdf)
3. **Read Write Inc.** (Ruth Miskin, Oxford University Press) — UK's most
   adopted commercial programme; structures 44 sounds into Set 1, 2, 3.
   [Read Write Inc parent guide (Oxford Owl)](https://home.oxfordowl.co.uk/reading/reading-schemes-oxford-levels/read-write-inc-phonics-guide/)
4. **Science of Reading consensus** as summarised by Reading Rockets and
   Keys to Literacy.
   [Reading Rockets — Phonics Instruction: The Basics](https://www.readingrockets.org/topics/phonics-and-decoding/articles/phonics-instruction-basics)
   [Keys to Literacy — Systematic Phonics Scope and Sequence (PDF)](https://www.readingrockets.org/sites/default/files/2023-10/Keys%20to%20Literacy%20Systematic-Phonics-Scope-and-Sequence.pdf)

All four frameworks broadly agree on the **shape** of the progression even
where they disagree on the precise letter-by-letter order. The 10 modules
below preserve that consensus shape and pick a specific letter-set order from
Letters and Sounds, the most commonly cited reference in the Science of
Reading literature.

## The phonological awareness foundation (before any letters)

Children cannot phonics until they can *hear* phonemes. The research-backed
hierarchy of skills, in order:

> Rhyming → syllable awareness → onset-rime → phoneme isolation → phoneme
> blending → phoneme segmenting → phoneme manipulation.
> ([Reading Rockets — The Development of Phonological Skills](https://www.readingrockets.org/topics/developmental-milestones/articles/development-phonological-skills))

For ages 3–4, the first three are the priority. Phonics work begins in
parallel around age 4–5 once a child can clap syllables and hear rhymes.

## The 10 Modules

Each module names the framework phase or set it derives from, the skill it
teaches, and the interactive technique used in the artifact.

### Module 1 · Listen & Rhyme  ·  *Phase 1, phonological awareness*
**Skill:** rhyming awareness — the gateway phonological skill.
**Interaction:** tap word pairs; the artifact says both words and asks
whether they rhyme.
**Why first:** rhyming is the earliest reliably-acquired phonological skill
and Phase 1 sets the foundation for everything that follows
(Letters and Sounds, p. 19).

### Module 2 · First Sounds: s, a, t, p  ·  *Phase 2, Set 1*
**Skill:** four letter-sound correspondences.
**Why these four:** the Letters and Sounds framework introduces s, a, t, p
in week one because together they form *sat, pat, tap, sap, at* — children
can read real CVC words within days. ([Phonics Family — Phases of Phonics](https://phonicsfamilycom.wordpress.com/phases-of-phonics/))

### Module 3 · More Sounds: i, n, m, d  ·  *Phase 2, Set 2*
**Skill:** four more sound-symbol pairings, including a second vowel.
**Why these four:** with i, n, m, d added, the available CVC pool jumps to
*sit, sat, pin, tin, mat, mad, dim, dad, sad…* — many more decodable words.

### Module 4 · Building Words I (CVC Blending)  ·  *Phase 2, mid*
**Skill:** synthetic phonics — *blending* individual phonemes into a whole
word.  The most important phonics skill in the research: *"phonemic
awareness…is the most important phonological element for the development of
reading and spelling"* ([Reading Rockets — Phonological Awareness](https://www.readingrockets.org/topics/phonological-and-phonemic-awareness/articles/phonological-awareness-instructional-and)).
**Interaction:** tap each letter card to hear its sound, then tap the whole
word to hear it blended.
**Words used:** sat, pin, mat, dim, did, mad, sad, tip — all from Sets 1+2.

### Module 5 · More Sounds: g, o, c, k  ·  *Phase 2, Set 3*
**Skill:** four more letter-sounds; introduces a second short vowel /o/.
**Note:** c and k make the same sound /k/ — the artifact tells the child
this explicitly. (Letters and Sounds, p. 56–58.)

### Module 6 · Find the First Sound  ·  *Phoneme isolation game*
**Skill:** *phoneme isolation* — given a target sound, identify the picture
whose name begins with it. This is the phonemic-awareness skill that most
strongly predicts later reading success.
([Reading Rockets — Phonological Awareness](https://www.readingrockets.org/topics/phonological-and-phonemic-awareness/articles/phonological-awareness-instructional-and))
**Interaction:** narrator says a phoneme; child taps the matching picture.

### Module 7 · More Sounds: ck, e, u, r, h, b, f, l  ·  *Phase 2, Sets 4–5*
**Skill:** the rest of Phase 2 — adds short e, short u, plus several
consonants and the first digraph (*ck* as in *duck*).
**Why grouped:** completes Phase 2 of Letters and Sounds, after which the
child knows enough sounds to read most simple CVC words in English.

### Module 8 · Tricky Words  ·  *Phase 2/3 sight words*
**Skill:** memorise high-frequency words with irregular spellings that
cannot be sounded out (*the, I, to, no, go, was, my, you, are, all*).
**Why:** ~50% of all English text is made up of the most common 100 words;
many of them are not phonetically regular.
([Read Write Inc — Set 1 sounds & tricky words](https://home.oxfordowl.co.uk/reading/reading-schemes-oxford-levels/read-write-inc-phonics-guide/))
**Interaction:** tap a word, hear it, repeat.

### Module 9 · More Sounds: j, v, w, x, y, z, qu  ·  *Phase 3 single letters*
**Skill:** the remaining single letters — completes the alphabet.
**Note on x and qu:** x = /ks/ (as in box, fox); qu = /kw/ (as in queen).
The artifact teaches these explicitly.

### Module 10 · Two Letters, One Sound (Digraphs: sh, ch, th, ng)  ·  *Phase 3*
**Skill:** consonant digraphs — two letters that team up to make a single
new sound. The first foray into "more than one letter per phoneme."
**Why digraphs before consonant blends:** *"a digraph represents one new
sound, which is a more concrete concept than a blend, where two sounds are
pushed together while remaining distinct."*
([Lead in Literacy — How to Teach Blends and Digraphs](https://leadinliteracy.com/how-to-teach-blends-and-digraphs/))

## Bonus (when the child is ready, after Module 10)

Modules 11+ would move into Phase 3 vowel teams (ai, ee, igh, oa, oo),
Phase 4 consonant blends (st, bl, tr, fr, cl…), and Phase 5 alternative
spellings (ay, ea, oy, magic-e). Those are typically taught in Year 1
(ages 5–6+), past the immediate scope of this artifact.

## The pedagogy across all 10 modules

Every module follows the same evidence-based shape:

1. **Multisensory introduction** — the child *sees* the letter, *hears* the
   pure phoneme (not the letter name), and *does* a body action when
   relevant. ([Continental Press — Science of Reading + 7 Activities](https://www.continentalpress.com/blog/science-of-reading-phonics/))
2. **Tap-only interaction** — single-tap to hear, no drag-and-drop, designed
   for 3-year-old motor skills.
3. **Examples baked in** — every letter card shows the letter, the pure
   sound, and one example word ("a as in ant").
4. **Free play, no fail states** — research is consistent that early
   readers quit when they are gated. The artifact never blocks progress.
